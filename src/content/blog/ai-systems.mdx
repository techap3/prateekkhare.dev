# Why Most AI Products Fail in Production

Most AI products fail not because the model is bad — they fail because the people building them treat non-determinism like a bug to be fixed rather than a constraint to be designed around.

> AI doesn’t fail in production. Systems do.

## The Demo Is Not the Product

A demo is a best-case scenario with a sympathetic user. Production is adversarial, repetitive, and full of edge cases the model has never seen. The moment you hand it to real users, the model's confidence scores mean nothing. What matters is whether the system recovers gracefully when the model is wrong.

## Trust Is Non-Linear

Users don't gradually lose trust in AI products. They lose it all at once — one wrong answer at the wrong moment, and the product is dead to them. This means <strong>reliability isn't a nice-to-have, it's load-bearing</strong>. A 95% accuracy rate sounds impressive until it's wrong 1 in 20 times in a high-stakes workflow.

## Safety Systems Are Invisible Until They're Not

The teams that ship AI products that last are obsessed with guardrails: <strong>idempotency, rollback, confirmation flows, explicit overrides</strong>. These aren't defensive pessimism. They're what separates a product from a prototype. If your AI feature has no circuit breaker, it's not a product.

## Deterministic First, AI Second

The best AI-assisted products I've seen treat the AI layer as an enhancement, not the foundation. They define what the system must do correctly every time, then layer AI on top for the parts where speed or scale matters more than perfection. <strong>The core remains deterministic.</strong>

## The Feedback Loop Is the Product

Most teams instrument for errors. The best teams instrument for drift — subtle changes in how the model behaves as inputs shift over time. Without a feedback loop that catches degradation before users do, you're flying blind. The feedback loop isn't a feature. It's the product.
